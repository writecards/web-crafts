Shoshana Zuboff is an American author and professor at Harvard University. She defines surveillance capitalism as “as the unilateral claiming of private human experience as free raw material for translation into behavioral data. These data are then computed and packaged as prediction products and sold into behavioral futures markets — business customers with a commercial interest in knowing what we will do now, soon, and later.” A key distinction about surveillance capitalism, is that the emphasis is on user’s behavioral data — google started mining how users type in the search bar for instance (capitalization, typos, etc) to predict the users emotions or needs. What’s alarming to me is how successful these companies are, as we are inherently used to a level of surveillance on the internet. Getting targeted ads on social media is something I’m sure a lot of us are accustomed to. I can’t help thinking about BeReal from a surveillance capitalism lens. BeReal, an app that promotes itself as being the most authentic social media app by prompting users to take. a photo at a random time of day, is actually the jackpot for surveillance capitalist corporations, as they can literally see how people are acting and what they’re doing at any time of day. It’s quite disturbing :0 Personally, maybe I’m cynical or hypocritical — but despite knowing the implications of corporate capitalism, I still partake from time to time. I feel similarly about surveillance capitalism, I know it’s happening, but feel a bit hopeless or complicit as someone whose data is being mined.

Adding on after the in-class discussion, perhaps it's Zuboff's tone that makes me feel this lack of agency. Because actually while I'm aware of surveillance capitalism, I think my day to day isn't impacted as much by it.
